IIT Bombay (Indian Institute of Technology 
Bombay) is a public engineering and technology 
institution in Mumbai, India. Founded in 1958, it 
is one of the most prestigious engineering 
institutes in India and is consistently ranked 
among the top institutes in the country. The 
campus of IIT Bombay is situated in the Powai 
area of Mumbai, spread over 550 acres. It is 
surrounded by lush green hills, lakes, and the 
Sanjay Gandhi National Park, offering a natural 
and serene environment for its diverse cohort of 
13,000+ students.

The institute hosts fifteen academic 
departments, two schools, four interdisciplinary 
programs, and thirty-seven centres that offer 
quality and holistic education. Several of these 
focus on sustainability, including the 
Environmental Science and Engineering 
Department, the Centre for Technology 
Alternatives for Rural Areas, and the Department 
of Energy Science and Engineering. More than 
680 faculty members, who are not only among 
the top in the nation but also highly regarded 
internationally for accomplishments in education 
and research, serve the institution.

Sustainability at IIT Bombay has been a 
prime area of interest since its 
establishment.  Through the years, the 
institute has demonstrated a solid 
commitment to creating and balancing a 
comfortable campus along with preserving 
the environment. We have made significant 
efforts towards sustainability in various 
areas like energy, water, waste 
management, buildings, transportation, and 
education, and remain committed to 
reducing our environmental impact and 
promoting sustainable practices on 
campus.

There are numerous student-led initiatives 
promoting sustainability at IIT Bombay. 
These include the Sustainability Cell, 
Abhyuday (social impact), Team Zero 
Waste (scalable waste management), Team 
SHUNYA (affordable, net positive energy 
housing), Energy Club (transitioning to 
sustainable energy through competitions, 
events, projects, and courses), and the 
National Service Scheme (educational 
outreach, environmental conservation, and 
sustainable social development)..

The institute's commitment to sustainability is 
evident in its campus infrastructure and 
operations. IIT Bombay has implemented various 
eco-friendly initiatives such as solar power 
generation, rainwater harvesting systems, 
energy-efficient buildings, and waste 
segregation programs. These efforts have not 
only reduced the institute's carbon footprint but 
also served as a model for sustainable practices 
in the academic community.

Furthermore, IIT Bombay actively collaborates 
with industry partners, government agencies, 
and non-profit organizations to drive sustainable 
innovation and research. Through partnerships 
and joint initiatives, the institute fosters 
knowledge exchange, technological 
advancements, and policy advocacy in areas like 
renewable energy, climate resilience, sustainable 
urban development, and environmental 
conservation. This collaborative approach 
reflects IIT Bombay's broader vision of 
contributing meaningfully to societal and 
environmental well-being through education, 
research, and outreach endeavors.

Editorial Note
We at the Sustainability Cell, IIT Bombay, are immensely proud to bring to 
you our first-ever Sustainability Development Goals Report. This document 
is a testament to our firm commitment to creating a healthier planet and 
better living conditions for all its inhabitants. It highlights our student 
activities, research and development, and administrative policies that are 
aligned with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (UN SDGs) 
- a set of 17 global goals adopted by all UN member states that aim to end 
poverty, protect the planet, and ensure peace and prosperity for everyone 
by 2030. 

As the world grapples with complex challenges like the COVID-19 
pandemic, climate change, food insecurity, and armed conflict, we 
understand that sustainability is not just about protecting the environment. 
It is about creating a world where everyone can thrive. This report 
showcases our efforts to promote sustainable practices in all aspects of our 
operations.0

change at IIT Bombay. We aim to achieve this by promoting behavioural, 
technological, and policy changes in the institute. We are raising awareness 
about sustainability through student engagement via events & competitions, 
ushering in efficient technologies like motion sensor lighting & waterless 
urinals, and collaborating with other institute bodies, staff, and students to 
achieve our goals.

1: Social Equality and 
Empowerment
IIT Bombay is committed to promoting inclusivity 
and providing equal opportunities for everyone, 
regardless of gender, race, or financial 
background. There are admission reservations for 
females, people with disability, and students from 
economically disadvantaged or historically 
marginalised communities. Additionally, the 
institute offers numerous scholarships and fee 
remittances on a merit-cum-means basis, 
ensuring that every student has the chance to 
succeed without having to worry about monetary 
constraints. In the last year alone, over INR 30 
crore was awarded in scholarships, along with 
laptops and other necessary technological 
devices. Furthermore, tuition, housing, and food 
are kept extremely affordable, significantly 
cheaper than market rates. 

Abhyuday, a student body promoting social 
welfare, has organised several career guidance 
sessions and learning programs for 
underprivileged school children. It has also 
conducted ten food distribution campaigns in the 
last year, providing 2000 meals to those in need. 
The Centre for Technology Alternatives for Rural 
Areas (CTARA) has been developing socially 
inclusive and environmentally sustainable 
technologies to improve the quality of life of 
marginalised rural communities. They accomplish 
this through participatory and collaborative 
processes that involve local knowledge, values, 
and aspirations. 

The institute has a Gender Cell and an SC/ST 
Cell, which mandate zero gender-based and 
caste-based discrimination, respectively. These 
bodies champion diversity and inclusion through 
their initiatives and act as forums for progressive 
debates. Saathi, the LGBTQ+ resource group, is 
constantly working towards creating a supportive 
and inclusive environment for students and staff 
of diverse sexual orientations and gender 
identities. The institute has also introduced 
‘GC101’, a gender sensitisation course 
compulsory for all students.

     Research and Development

IIT Bombay researchers have been contributing 
to Social Equality and Empowerment in numerous 
ways. Their work has focused on financial 
inclusion, socio-economic vulnerability to climate 
change, women empowerment, and upliftment of 
farmers, among other fields. Noteworthy 
agricultural projects include the fabrication of 
anti-hail guns to protect crops from hailstorms, 
and the development of plant-level stress maps

to many Indian languages, helping break down 
linguistic barriers.

Executive 
Summary

This report has been drafted keeping in mind 
the 17 UN Sustainable Development Goals 
(SDGs) and their 169 target indicators. 
Naturally, it often becomes difficult to 
comprehend the overall picture while 
examining individual SDGs, especially due to 
their interconnectedness. To make the 
executive summary more digestible, we 
group the SDGs into ‘pillars’ of sustainability, 
linked to each other through the ‘Overall 
Sustainability Framework’. The framework 
envisages people at the centre, with the 17 
SDGs meaningfully integrated into 6 pillars - 
each contributing to enhancing the 
development process.

2: Access to Basic 
Needs

IIT Bombay has harnessed its proficiency in 
innovation and technology to significantly 
augment the accessibility of fundamental 
necessities for all. Notably, the institute houses a 
hospital with skilled medical professionals, 
accessible to all members of the institute at a 
nominal cost. The Koita Centre for Digital Health 
(KCDH) set up at the institute is the first of its 
kind in India, focused on driving academic 
programs, research, and industry collaborations 
in Digital Health. Moreover, the institute has 
implemented exemplary mentorship models and 
student assistance systems, namely the Institute 
Student Mentorship Program (ISMP), Department 
Academic Mentorship Program (DAMP), and 
Student Wellness Centre (SWC), to ensure the 
holistic development of its students.


IIT Bombay has established a remarkable 
reputation for providing an unparalleled and 
superlative educational experience, helping it 
consistently rank among India’s best universities 
in multiple prestigious university rankings. The 
institute has earned the distinction of being the 
first public institution in the country to establish a 
centre for liberal education, enabling students to 
explore their interests and design a tailored 
concentration while receiving necessary 
guidance. Additionally, the institute offers various 
courses and research opportunities specialising 
in sustainability, including an integrated 
bachelor's and master's program in technology.

     
IIT Bombay has developed in-house innovative 
solutions to a plethora of perils faced in the 
healthcare industry.  Technologies like automatic 
radiology report generation from X-ray, MRI, CT, 
and ultrasound plates, an indigenous and 
affordable CAR-T cell platform to treat blood 
cancers, implantable electrocorticography 
(ECoG) probes for neuro-surgery and neuro-
monitoring, and a 3-D scaffold matrix & 
bioresorbable screws for bone grafting and 
reconstruction have entirely revolutionised 
research in the healthcare industry.

Research and Development

The Centre for Technology Alternatives for Rural 
Areas (CTARA) at IIT Bombay has done 
exemplary work in identifying the sources of 
contamination for rural water and effectively 
addressing the same. CTARA has also conducted 
several training programs for rural communities 
to enhance their knowledge about the 
significance of clean drinking water and its 
maintenance. 

Moreover, the institute has employed an 
innovative Integrated Wetland Technology (IWT)

manner.


3: Sustainable Living

IIT Bombay has undertaken numerous initiatives 
towards achieving sustainable living, promoting 
energy efficiency, clean energy, and sustainable 
production and consumption practices. To ensure 
energy efficiency, the institute has installed 
modern electric equipment, sensor-based 
lighting, waterless urinals, and solar water 
heaters in student hostels, and technologies like 
timed remote switches for lab equipment, among 
others. The Department of Energy Science & 
Engineering organizes an annual Energy Day 
event that showcases the latest research and 
innovations related to energy systems. The 
institute has also launched the Energy Swaraj 
Club, which works towards realizing energy 
security and climate change mitigation in India.

Various student bodies are striving to make a 
positive impact on the campus population by 
encouraging people to incorporate sustainability 
into daily decision-making. The Sustainability Cell 
organized Airathon Mumbai, the world's second-
largest air quality modeling competition, and is 
launching IIT Bombay’s first-ever ‘Green Cup’, to 
be awarded to the hostel generating the smallest 
carbon footprint per capita. Team SHUNYA stood 
second globally in the US Solar Decathlon Build 
Challenge 2023, having secured USD 50,000 
funding for developing net-zero housing. The 
Energy Club offered courses on global strategy, 
international relations, management, and 
fundamental energy as part of their Technical 
Summer School 2022.

IIT Bombay has also taken initiatives to promote 
sustainable production and consumption 
practices on its campus. Waste segregation in 
campus hostels has been implemented to 
separate waste into three categories: wet 
(compostable) waste, dry (non-recyclable) 
waste, and recyclable waste, with educational 
initiatives such as speaker sessions on the 
recycling process. The campus also has several 
buildings with sensor-based lighting and water-
efficient sprinkler tap systems to cut down on the 
wastage of electricity and water. The institute 
has an effective waste management system that 
diverts most of the waste from reaching landfills, 
employing a biogas plant and composting 
facilities to treat biodegradable waste.
     
Several professors are conducting research on 
cleaner energy and renewables, energy 
efficiency, carbon capture, and climate change 
mitigation using technological interventions. 
Some innovative projects include a 100-kWh solid 
state hydrogen storage device, sustainable and 
cost-effective Na-ion battery systems, natural 
sunlight-driven hydrogen generation from water, 
an integrated municipal solid waste management 
system, biogas-enriching CO₂/CH₄ separation 
membranes, and a decentralized onsite sewage 
treatment plant.

5: Holistic Environmental 
Protection

IIT Bombay is actively engaged in preserving and 
safeguarding the environment with a focus on 
addressing climate change, reducing waste, 
cleaning up polluted water sources, and adopting 
pollution prevention methods. The administration 
and student bodies are making substantial efforts 
towards achieving a climate-positive campus.

NSS has organised conservation activities such as 
tree plantation drives, green Diwali campaigns, and 
paper recycling drives to improve air quality, protect 
biodiversity, and promote an eco-friendly campus. 
Talks on waste reduction in critical regions of India 
are also being conducted, with the aim of promoting 
eco-friendliness. Abhyuday has initiated a 
nationwide tree plantation drive called “Grow Green 
Gold” to combat deforestation.


Additionally, it conducts regular clean-up drives 
at lakes and beaches, collecting a large quantity 
of debris, thereby educating people about the 
impact of pollution and encouraging them to 
adopt sustainable practices to reduce their 
environmental footprint.

The institute has also introduced electric buggies 
for eco-friendly transportation to lower its carbon 
footprint and promote sustainability. The 
Industrial Design Centre (IDC) in collaboration 
with NSS, has been promoting the use of eco-
friendly materials for making Ganesha idols for 
the past 20 years. This initiative prevents toxic 
paint materials from contaminating water bodies 
during the immersion of the idols.
Abhyuday's efforts extend to the preservation 
of local ecosystems through regular clean-up 
drives at Powai Lake and Versova Beach. These 
initiatives are integral to raising awareness 
among the institute community and the public 
about environmental conservation. The Powai 
Lake Mega clean-up, with over 800 volunteers, 
resulted in the collection of more than 2.5 
tonnes of solid waste along the lake's banks, 
illustrating the significant impact of community-
driven environmental initiatives. These activities 
not only contribute to preserving natural 
resources but also educate individuals on the 
importance of sustainable practices for 
reducing pollution and maintaining a cleaner 
environment.

increasing awareness on climate change and its 
consequences through panel discussions and 
science-based competitions.

Development and evaluation of 
indigenous anti-hail guns

Prof. Sudarshan Kumar, an Aerospace 
Engineering professor, and his team have 
developed an indigenous anti-hail gun to protect 
crops from hailstorms in Himachal Pradesh, India. 
This region, known as the fruit basket of India, 
suffers from crop damage due to heavy 
hailstorms every year. The anti-hail gun uses 
strong shock waves that neutralize the clouds' 
positive and negative charges, preventing the 
formation of hail by stopping water droplets from 
freezing. The gun is cost-effective, eco-friendly, 
and has low maintenance costs, making it an 
excellent alternative to plastic anti-hail nets 
currently in use. The anti-hail gun will generate 
employment and revenue for local people and 
industries, and the product is expected to be in 
high demand in the region. A fully functional anti-
hail gun is currently installed at Krishi Vigyan 
Kendra, Kandaghat, District Solan, and 
experimental data is being collected to improve 
its efficiency.
Drones for more crop per drop

Prof. J. Adinarayana from the Centre for Studies 
in Resources Engineering has developed a 
solution to address the challenges faced by 
Indian farmers due to traditional agricultural 
practices and climate change. By using science-
based and machine-learning approaches on 
hyperspectral data, the technique he has devised 
creates plant-level stress maps that enable 
farmers to distinguish between crop water and 
nitrogen stress. This will help farmers make 
informed decisions regarding resource input for 
healthy farming practices. The technique has 
been validated multiple times and is ready to be 
used as decision support system software for 
farmers. The solution will reduce groundwater 
pollution by minimizing nitrate-nitrogen losses

enable mass transfer among molecules, resulting 
in a short reaction time. The optimized flow 
reactor method can prepare complex oxazole 
compounds on a kilogram scale with minimal 
post-synthetic work-up. The technology has 
successfully synthesized oxazole molecules with 
readily available nitrile sources, targeting a novel 
pathway that could help control the epidemic of 
obesity, diabetes, and accompanying maladies. 
Thin film silicon piezoresistive

Prof. Rajiv O. Dusane at the Department of 
Metallurgical Engineering & Materials Science 
has developed a solution for the critical 
problem of foot ulcers caused by peripheral 
neuropathy in diabetic patients in India, which 
can lead to amputation of a toe or foot. The 
proposed solution is a thin film piezoresistive 
pressure sensor that can check foot pressure 
in a cost-effective manner. The uniqueness of 
the solution lies in the use of aluminum 
induced crystallization (AIC) to fabricate 
microcrystalline silicon on flexible substrates, 
reducing the fabrication cost. The technology 
has been validated in a laboratory 
environment, and it offers a more affordable 
diagnostic solution for the Indian population, 
preventing the leads to pain, loss of 
productivity and social role, unemployment, 
increased dependency of patients, and stigma 
due to distorted body image caused due to 
amputation.
Sodium potassium electrofinder

Professor Rohit Srivastava and their team at the 
Department of Biosciences and Bioengineering 
have developed a compact and cost-effective 
Blood Electrolyte quantification device that 
quantifies sodium and potassium in human blood 
samples. The device utilizes turbidimetry and 
nephelometry principles to quantify the 
electrolytes present in the sample. The device is 
unique in that it uses a point-of-care optical 
reader implemented on the principle of 
absorbance and scattering of light in liquid 
solutions. The prototype has been tested with a 
large number of clinical samples at the KEM 
Hospital, Mumbai. The proposed technology will 
reduce the cost of sodium and potassium blood 
tests by 50 INR per test and requires only 10µl of 
serum sample and 60 seconds per test, making it 
useful in facilities with limited resources such as 
primary health centers, ambulance services, 
disaster sites and rural healthcare centers. 
Small animal cerebral blood flow 
imaging platform

Professor Hari M Varma and their team at the 
Department of Biosciences and Bioengineering 
have developed a small animal cerebral blood 
flow imaging platform based on a new method 
called Multi speckle diffuse correlation 
spectroscopy (M-DCS). The system, which 
comprises a laser and a camera, can capture 
intensity speckles and post-process them to 
obtain 2-D and 3-D cerebral blood flow 
distributions. This cost-effective and compact 
deep tissue imaging system can be used for 
monitoring the effects of drugs on post-stroke 
reperfusion or functional activation studies in 
small animal models. It is also being scaled to 
adapt for longitudinal bedside monitoring of 
cerebral blood flow in human patients. The low-
cost technology has the potential to boost 
research work for drug discovery/testing and 
functional activation/cognitive studies in 
academia and industry, as well as for preclinical 
studies towards drug developments for diseases 
like Parkinson’s, Alzheimer’s, and stroke.

monitoring.

standard intervention protocols, and implemented 
them on a pilot basis.

assessment has been instrumental in improving 
access to safe and clean drinking water in rural 
areas. Their initiatives have been aimed at 
improving the access to and quality of drinking 
water in rural areas, which is crucial for the health

research has contributed significantly to the

Integrated Wetland Technology

The institute employs Integrated Wetland 
Technology (IWT), an innovative natural treatment 
system for sewage that overcomes the limitations 
of conventional sewage treatment plants by using 
wetland vegetation and microbial assemblages to 
treat wastewater. It consists of primary, 
secondary, and tertiary treatment systems and a 
two-stage sludge management system that 
makes it more efficient than most wetland-based 
technologies. The final treated water meets 
discharge standards set by the Central Pollution 
Control Board (CPCB) and can be used for 
secondary purposes. The IWT is technologically 
feasible and economically viable, conserves 
energy, and promotes a transition to a low-carbon 
economy.
SafeSan: Safe sanitation system

Developed by Prof. Anil Kumar Dikshit, SafeSan is 
a cost-effective, efficient, and low-land 
requirement system that can be used as a 
prefabricated isolated toilet unit in individual 
households, toilet blocks at the community level, 
and public conveniences at bus stops, railway 
stations, and highways. An innovative 
decentralized sewage treatment unit, Safesan is a 
solution to the sanitation crisis in India where 
more than 50% of the population lacks household 
toilets, and nearly 70% of sewage generated goes 
untreated. The treatment unit has an organic and 
solid removal efficiency of 85% and has been 
successfully implemented in residential and 
commercial establishments. SafeSan is in line with 
the objectives of Swachh Bharat Abhiyaan and 
has the potential to provide clean and safe access 
to toilets in public places, rural areas, slums, and 
construction sites.

organized the Powai Lake Cleanup Drive on June 
4, 2023, aligning with World Environment Day. The 
initiative witnessed a substantial turnout of 400 
volunteers, comprising students, faculty, and 
staff. The dedicated team diligently cleared 3 
tonnes of debris from the lake's 2 km perimeter 
and shoreline, advocating for a cleaner, greener 
environment. The collected waste was 
responsibly transported for recycling by Project 
Mumbai. The campaign addressed the issue of 
insufficient dustbins along the promenade, 
leading to plastic litter, a concern conveyed to 
government officials. This project was in 
collaboration with UNICEF India and Project 
Mumbai. The aim of the campaign was to convey 
that individual actions, no matter how small, hold 
significant power to fulfil the responsibility 
towards creating plastic-free environments.

High Flux Anti-fouling HFM for 
Water Treatment Application

The research conducted by Professor Jayesh 
Bellare and their team at IIT Bombay aims to 
address the fouling-related issues in wastewater 
treatment through the development of a high flux 
anti-fouling HFM using hydrophilic additives in 
polyethersulfone. This innovative solution of 
embedding carboxylated multi-walled carbon 
nanotubes (MWCNTs-COOH) in hollow fibre 
membranes (HFMs) can remarkably enhance their 
permeation flux and fouling-resistance. The 
developed PES composite HFMs containing TPGS 
and MWCNTs-COOH have shown excellent 
antifouling properties, making them potentially 
useful for wastewater treatment. The proposed 
low-cost solution for filtration of domestic 
wastewater can provide water for non-drinking 
applications, thereby addressing the pressing 
issue of water scarcity in the country. This 
development can positively impact industries 
related to the environment, water treatment, 
effluent treatment plants, and materials, and also 
contribute to the sustainable development of 
society.

Photocatalytic Membrane Reactor 
for Water Treatment

Professor Jayesh Bellare and their team have 
developed a promising solution to tackle the issue 
of water pollution caused by dyes and 
pharmaceutical compounds. The team has 
developed a photocatalytic membrane reactor 
that uses photocatalysts in the presence of UV or 
sunlight to degrade organic compounds, coupled 
with a cost-effective hollow fiber membrane to 
remove dyes and pharmaceutical compounds 
from contaminated water. This technology has the 
potential to provide a low-cost solution to Indian 
industries for treating effluent water and making 
clean water more accessible. Currently, the 
technology is in the preliminary stages, with 
laboratory trials being performed. This innovative 
technology could have a significant societal 
impact by improving the quality of water and 
mitigating the negative environmental impact 
caused by these contaminants. 
Mobile Water Treatment Plant 
(mWTP) for Emergency Situations

Professor Anil Kumar Dikshit has led a team of 
researchers in designing a mobile water treatment 
plant system for emergency situations. The 
proposed solution has a unique two-stage 
treatment approach, which includes a decision 
support system and a water quality testing 
system. Depending on the water quality, the DSS 
recommends if the water should be treated in one 
or two stages, with treatment options including 
coagulation, sedimentation, filtration, ozonation, 
membrane filtration, and chlorination. The system 
has the potential to reduce health problems in the 
aftermath of disasters like floods and storms.
U-bent optical fiber sensors

Professor Soumyo Mukherji from the Biosciences 
and Bioengineering department has developed a 
sensor that can detect bacteria, viruses, heavy

Green Sewage Treatment Plant 
Based on Integrated Wetland 
Technology

alternative solution to conventional Sewage 
Treatment Plants (STP) that can address the

This new technology is relevant to municipalities, 
cities, and towns, and can have a positive impact 
on the environment.

Biogas Enriching CO₂ /CH₄ 
Separation Membranes

Professor Jayesh Bellare of Chemical Engineering 
has developed a technology to remove CO₂ from 
biogas using a combination of membranes and 
zeolitic imidazolate frameworks-67 (ZIF-67). 
Biogas is a renewable energy source, but it 
contains CO₂, which contaminates the gas and 
reduces its efficiency. The technology uses 
hollow fiber membranes coated with ZIF-67 
nanoparticles to separate CO₂ and CH₄. The
experiments conducted have shown that the 
modified membranes could be useful for
practical gas separation applications. The 
technology has the potential to enrich biogas
by removing CO₂, thereby improving its efficiency 
and making it a more cost-effective fuel source. 
The technology has a significant societal impact, 
as it can promote the use of clean energy for 
domestic and industrial purposes, reducing 
greenhouse gas emissions, and fuel costs. 
Additionally, the technology aligns with the Indian
government's SATAT initiative (Sustainable 
Alternative Towards Affordable Transportation) to 
promote the use of compressed biogas for 
automotive fuels. This innovation can play a 
critical role in the transition towards a sustainable 
energy future, reducing our dependence on non-
renewable energy sources.

Fabrication of Crystalline Silicon-
Based Heterojunction (SHJ) Thin-
Film Solar Cells

Professor Rajiv O. Dusane and their team at the 
Metallurgical Engineering and Materials Science 
department have developed a low-cost, high-
efficiency solar cell that could revolutionize 
renewable energy. The Fabrication of Crystalline 
Silicon-Based Heterojunction (SHJ) Thin-Film 
Solar Cells uses the heterojunction with intrinsic 
thin film (HIT) concept and hot-wire chemical 
vapour deposition (HWCVD) to deposit all silicon 
layers, making the fabrication process industry-
friendly and easy to upscale. The SHJ solar cells 
do not require photolithography and use low-
temperature processing, reducing both material 
and energy costs. The availability of these solar 
panels can improve the lives of people in rural 
areas where electricity supply is inconsistent. The 
SHJ solar cells can be used for rooftop energy 
generation, agriculture, irrigation demands, and 
transportation. This innovation represents a 
significant step forward in renewable energy, with 
the potential to impact communities worldwide.

Natural sunlight-driven H2

Professors Arnab Dutta, Debabrata Maiti, and 
Vikram Vishal are developing a sustainable and 
scalable hydrogen-gas generator that converts 
solar energy into hydrogen gas to produce 
electricity. The generator will function in neutral 
water without using hazardous solvents or acids, 
making it an environmentally-friendly and energy-
efficient solution for reducing carbon dioxide 
emissions. The team has validated their proof-of-

systems. This technology has potential for 
application in the power and energy, petroleum, 
and chemical industries, offering a path towards 
carbon-neutral energy infrastructure.

Rechargeable metal-air batteries

Professor Arindam Sarkar and their team have 
developed a new solution to improve the 
rechargeability of metal-air batteries, which can 
store more electricity than lithium-ion batteries. 
However, metal-air batteries have problems with 
the reversibility of reactions at both the metal 
and air electrodes. To solve this, the team has 
designed a hybrid electrolyte that allows for 
good reversibility at both electrodes. Their 
design decouples the reactions, making it 
possible to use aqueous electrolytes for the air 
electrode and an aprotic environment for the 
metal electrode. The team has created a small 
cell and a lab-scale prototype and filed for 
patents. This technology has significant societal 
impact, enabling high-voltage metal-air batteries 
for portable and stationary applications in 
industries such as batteries, clean energy, and 
renewables. This breakthrough can contribute to 
the transition to renewable energy, which relies 
heavily on efficient electricity storage.

Rapid, low-cost hydrogen 
production using magnets

Professor Chandramouli Subramaniam and their 
team have developed a solution to produce green 
hydrogen more efficiently and cost-effectively. 
Though electrolysis of water is a sustainable 
method, it is costly and requires a lot of energy. 
The team introduced a weak external magnetic 
field intl the electrolytic chamber and used earth-
abundant metal oxides (nickel, cobalt, and their 
combinations) as a catalyst, which has decreased 
energy consumption by 20% and increased 
hydrogen production three times. This technology 
can make India a leader in green hydrogen 
production and will significantly impact the steel 
industry, which relies on non-renewable sources 
of hydrogen. The team has successfully 
demonstrated a small-scale model and is seeking 
industry support to prototype and validate this 
technology commercially. This innovation has the 
potential to revolutionize the hydrogen storage, 
electric vehicle, clean energy, and renewable 
industries.

Customizable miniature wire EDM

Ultra high-speed micromachining 
center

Prof. Ramesh Kumar Singh of the Mechanical 
engineering department has developed an Ultra 
High-speed Micromachining Center that is the 
first of its kind in India. The machine is engineered 
to minimize vibrations, and chatter-induced tool 
failure has been addressed through predictive 
stability modeling. The machine can compete with 
state-of-the-art systems in Germany and 
Singapore and will increase the global 
competitiveness of the Indian industry in the 
micromanufacturing domain. The technology has 
the potential to create a vibrant micro-
manufacturing ecosystem in India and catalyze 
the development of machines and micro-
manufacturing services in the country. It could 
also increase employment opportunities. 
Microscale feature creation using micro-milling/
drilling is used in various fields such as 
lithography masks, micro-moulds, micro-needle 
arrays for drug delivery, microfluidics, and 
miniature heat-exchangers. However, due to the 
lack of technical know-how and prohibitively 
expensive solutions offered by German 
companies, there is very low penetration of this 
technology in India. The researchers hope to 
bridge this gap by providing ultra-high-speed 
technology solutions and fabrication services to 
the Indian industry and strategic sector.

Maximizing Computation in 
Blockchains

Professors Umesh Bellur and Vinay J. Ribeiro from the 
Computer Science and Engineering department have 
proposed an on-chain protocol to enable 
computation-intensive transactions (CIT) on 
blockchains, such as Ethereum, without compromising 
security. Currently, blockchains allocate only a tiny 
fraction of the block inter-arrival time for validating 
smart contracts present in transactions, which causes 
forking and increases unfairness. Offloading validation 
to non-chain nodes suffers from trust and 
performance issues. The researchers have developed 
a prototype implementation atop Ethereum, 
demonstrating scalability without the harmful effects 
of naïve scaling up of computation time. The solution 
will enable the integration of applications using heavy 
computation, including AI/ML and privacy-preserving 
cryptography, and benefit industries such as 
Blockchain, Software, Finance, and Banking. A patent 
has been filed, and the security analysis is ongoing.

DetFroSS: Frozen Soil Shear

Professor D.N. Singh from the Civil Engineering

strength, density, saturation, and moisture, which 
are essential in designing building foundations in 
different temperature zones. The Frozen Soil 
Shear Strength Estimator quickly and 
economically determines the shear strength of 
frozen soil and volumetric deformation that could 
impact the foundation during construction. The 
setup will be helpful in executing infrastructure 
development, mainly in Northeast India, where the 
soil temperature is up to 25℃. The technology will 
benefit ecologists and engineers to design 
buildings and infrastructure that can withstand 
harsh environmental circumstances, promoting 
the country's growth and development.

Integrated Municipal Solid Waste 
Management System (IMSWMS)

Professor Anil Kumar Dikshit has also developed 
an integrated municipal solid waste management 
system to address India's unscientific and 
chaotic collection, transportation, and disposal of 
municipal solid waste. The system involves 
segregating the waste into dry, inert, and organic 
waste, which is then compacted, recycled, 
repurposed, and bio-stabilized to act as a soil 
enhancer and bio-manure. The system has been 
successfully implemented by the Mira Bhayandar 
Municipal Corporation and the Naya Raipur 
Development Authority, leading to better solid 
waste collection and management, which 
reduces the problems caused by solid waste, 
such as groundwater pollution and contribution 
to global warming. Cities, towns, urban local 
bodies, and municipalities will benefit from this 
system.

StabMd: A Novel Technique to 
Stabilize Marine Deposits

Professor D N Singh has also developed a 
methodology called StabMd, which stabilizes 
marine deposits using industrial byproducts 
(IBPs). India faces scarcity of land for 
infrastructure facilities, and marine deposits, like 
clay and sediments, are under-consolidated and 
difficult to plan for without stabilization. StabMd 
addresses these issues using human-made 
resources. StabMd is demonstrated in the in-situ 
condition to stabilize marine deposits at Navi 
Mumbai 4th container terminal. The use of IBPs 
for stabilization reduces dependence on natural 
resources and allows for bulk utilization of 
industrial byproducts, leading to lower pollution 
loads and overall sustainable development. A 
patent has been filed and the technology has 
potential applications for alumina refineries, 
alcofine manufacturers, thermal power plants, and 
geotechnical consultancy groups.

development and sustainable materials.

Waste management facilities

IIT Bombay has implemented an effective waste 
management system that diverts most of the 
waste from reaching landfills. The system 
employs a biogas plant and composting facilities 
to treat wet and dry biodegradable waste. The 
biogas plant uses anaerobic digestion to treat 
wet waste and produces methane and other 
gases that are used as a form of energy. The 
methane obtained is used as fuel for cooking in 
hostel messes, and the slurry is used as fertiliser 
for horticulture. Composting facilities treat dry 
biodegradable waste such as leaves and wet 
waste from the residential sector. The process 
involves adding the waste in a ventilated 
chamber or a pit and leaving it undisturbed for a 
specified period with tilling or mixing in known 
intervals. The facilities receive waste from staff 
quarters and residential buildings, except hostels. 
The process requires the maintenance of an 
adequate environment and is not carried out 
during the monsoon season. The resulting 
compost is used as fertiliser for horticulture 
within the campus.

STP-on-Wheels: Decentralized 
Onsite Sewage Treatment Plant

Prof. Anil Kumar Dikshit of the Environmental 
Science and Engineering Department is working 
on a novel and highly effective technology for on-
site and mobile treatment of municipal 
wastewater. A tremendous amount of wastewater 
is produced in cities and transported over great 
distances to traditional centralized wastewater 
treatment systems (CWTs), which causes several 
operating issues. In reality, the CWTs are often 
unable to handle significant sewage loads. As a 
result, the wastewater is occasionally dumped 
into water bodies without being fully treated, 
which has a negative impact on the environment. 
Prof. Dikshit is developing a potable mechanism 
for the safe and complete treatment of 
wastewater at source. The suggested system 
treats the wastewater in three different ways: i) 
through a centrifuge, producing treated water and 
biogas for reuse; ii) through a combination of 
aerobic, facultative, and anaerobic treatment; and 
iii) through a pre-settling tank and mechanical 
aeration unit. The project's primary advantage is 
the decrease in the harmful environmental effects 
caused by wastewater that hasn't been fully or 
adequately treated.

Removing CO2 from the 
Atmosphere

Professor Kamendra P. Sharma and his team from 
the Department of Chemistry have designed a 
composite porous liquid with honey-like 
rheological properties that can capture and store 
carbon dioxide (CO2) and catalytically transform it 
into components that are useful for the industrial 
world. This method minimizes the amount of CO2 
in the atmosphere, helps sequester it and 
transform it into useful chemicals and valuable 
goods like CaCO3 polymorphs, all of which 
benefit India and the rest of the globe. It has 
applications in the sectors of healthcare, 
pharmaceuticals, agriculture, households, and 
many industries such as cement, glass, steel, and 
paper. In addition to having a favorable influence 
on society as a whole, this technology can lessen 
the effects of climate change and global warming.
Multiport Controlled Hele-Shaw 
Cells for Multi-Scale 
Manufacturing

Professor Prasanna Gandhi from the Department 
of Mechanical Engineering is working on 
lithography-free fabrication techniques for 
various structures such as fractal geometry, 
regular array and large three-point boundary 
islands at multiple scales from sub-micron to 300 
cm. The proposed solution offers a 
comprehensive out-of-the-box solution for this 
multiscale fabrication. The method is based on 
the Saffman Taylor instability that occurs when 
plates are separated, and control is achieved by 
seeding the cell plates, creating preferred paths 
for the growth of fluid structures in the desired 
pattern. The technology has been tested in the 
laboratory and has been found to have 
applications in the field of energy for efficient 
hydrogen production. It has also been found to 
have utility in the healthcare sector for 
development of a whole blood plasma separator 
that produces high yields of 30 microliter blood 
injection volume. If successful, the proposed 
technology would have a positive social impact 
because it could facilitate the production of clean 
and efficient hydrogen and a high-throughput 
whole blood plasma separator of 30 microliter 
blood heel injection volume.

Tailored hollow fiber

Prof. Jayesh Bellare of the Chemical Engineering 
department has developed a solution to address

development issues, and increased cancer risk. 
Bellare's team has created tailored hollow fibre 
nanocomposite membranes for wastewater 
treatment that are efficient and low-fouling, 
addressing the two major problems associated 
with membrane technology: separation efficiency 
and antifouling. The technology has been 
demonstrated at lab scale and holds significant 
potential in the national and international market

invention offers a solution to remediate toxic 
water contaminants, directly impacting society's 
health.
Membrane sequential bio-reactor 
for decolorization of wastewater

Prof. Anil Kumar Dikshit of Environmental Science 
& Engineering has developed a unique Membrane 
Sequential Bioreactor (MSBR) in the 
Environmental Infrastructure and Clean 
Technologies Laboratory that can treat distillery 
wastewater in a cost-effective and eco-friendly 
manner. The MSBR process is capable of 
decolourising wastewater and reducing organic 
matter to permissible limits, resulting in a high-
quality effluent that meets stringent standards. 
The MSBR can work in a non-sterile environment 
and does not require any dilution of water, thus 
saving costs for industries that require large 
amounts of water. The technology has been 
extensively tested in lab-scale reactors, and the 
team aspires to implement a large prototype in an 
industrial setup. Distilleries, one of the most 
water-intensive industries, produces 8-15 litres of 
wastewater per litre of alcohol produced and 
often lacks sustainable wastewater solutions, 
making MSBR a viable solution for reducing 
freshwater requirements and safeguarding 
against pollution.

What is it?

At the heart of IIT Bombay's sustainability efforts 
lies the Green Cup, an annual competition 
orchestrated by the Sustainability Cell as their 
flagship event. This initiative taps into the 
competitive spirit inherent in hostel life, 
transforming sustainability into a vibrant and 
spirited contest. The Green Cup motivates 
hostels to compete in energy conservation, 
waste reduction, and the organization of 
sustainability-themed events. Beyond being a 
competition, it signifies a collective commitment 
to cultivating sustainable practices and fostering 
a sense of shared responsibility for a greener 
future. The Green Cup ranking will be done 
based on the calculation of the Green Cup Index. 
The Green Cup index is an index which will be 
calculated for each hostel which showcases how 
sustainable each hostel is in the domains of 
Waste, Electricity, and Energy. This index will be 
used to compare and rank hostels in the Green 
Cup General Championship.
The Green Cup
How did it come into being?

The concept of the Green Cup emerged as an 
initiative by the Sustainability Cell of IIT Bombay 
with an aim not only to perfect sustainability 
practices but also to instigate a campus-wide 
movement where every individual contributes to 
a more sustainable lifestyle. With low awareness 
of sustainability and insufficient engagement in 
eco-friendly practices, the event serves as a 
catalyst for raising awareness, promoting 
sustainable behavior, and fostering a more 
environmentally conscious campus culture. 
Beyond its environmental impact, the Green Cup 
provides a dynamic and enjoyable platform for 
students to actively contribute to sustainability 
and effect positive change in their community.

The launch of the Green Cup coincided with the 
Sustainability Cell's Orientation, which 
introduced newcomers to the cell's vision and 
mission. The Green Cup is an embodiment of our 
belief that behavioral change is pivotal for 
sustainable living on campus.
How it’s going..

Hostels are expected to actively engage in 
practices that contribute to their Green Cup 
rankings. From reducing mess food wastage and 
segregating waste efficiently to organizing 
sustainability-themed events and conserving 
electricity, every hostel plays a crucial role. The 
enthusiasm displayed during the launch reflects 
the campus's passion for positive environmental 
change.


In conclusion, the Green Cup stands as a beacon 
of sustainable practices at IIT Bombay, fostering 
a culture of responsible living and environmental 
awareness. The meticulous evaluation, driven by 
data from various sources and orchestrated by 
the Sustainability Cell, ensures a fair and 
competitive spirit. As the Green Cup unfolds, it 
not only propels sustainability forward but also 
showcases the collective efforts of the institute's 
community toward a greener and more 
conscious future.
In the Green Cup, hostels will be assessed 
monthly, receiving a 'Green Score' reflecting their 
sustainability performance. The winner is 
determined by the average of monthly scores. 
The inaugural edition runs from November 2023 
to April 2024, excluding December due to winter 
vacation. A dynamic live leaderboard on the 
Sustainability Cell's website ranks hostels based 
on their cumulative average scores and thus 
deciding the winner of the Green Cup.

The Sustainability Cell spearheads the Green 
Cup evaluation process, collaborating closely 
with the Public Health Office (PHO). The Cell's 
commitment to transparency ensures fair 
competition and underscores their pivotal role in 
the Green Cup's success.
What methodologies do we 
plan to use?

The different hostels participating in the Green 
Cup will be evaluated by the Sustainability Cell 
on parameters like mess food wastage, mess 
food segregation, hostel waste management, 
electricity usage, sustainability events, and 
attendance at the Green Cup Launch. Each 
parameter holds a specific weightage in the 
evaluation process, emphasizing aspects like 
mess food waste reduction, efficient 
segregation, electricity conservation, and active 
participation in sustainability events.

'Wasted Food in Mess' quantifies discarded food 
weight, emphasizing reduction in this significant 
source of waste. 'Mess Waste Segregation' 
targets proper classification of wet and non-wet 
waste, addressing challenges faced by biogas 
plant workers. 'Electricity Usage' encompasses 
total hostel electricity consumption for a month, 
sourced from EMD records. 'Hostel Waste' 
encompasses all non-mess waste generated 
within the hostel, fostering a holistic approach to 
waste management and sustainability.

Mess food wastage (20%) quantifies food 
wastage through data from the Public Health 
Office (PHO) which is collected and analysed 
meticulously by members of the operations 
subdivision of the Sustainability Cell.. Mess food 
segregation (15%) focuses on effective waste 
categorization. This targets proper classification 
of wet and non-wet waste, addressing 
challenges faced by biogas plant workers who 
have to manually separate the waste out. Hostel 
waste management (10%) assesses overall 
waste practices using PHO data. Electricity 
usage (30%) relies on rank-based scoring from 
the Electrical & Maintenance Division (EMD). 
Events/ZWDs (Zero Wastewater Discharge) 
(10%), which accounts for all non-mess waste 
generated within the hostel, rewards hostels for 
organizing monthly sustainability events. 
Attendance in the Green Cup Launch (5%) adds a 
participation element. This data-driven approach 
ensures a fair and transparent competition.

The weightages assigned to each parameter in 
the Green Cup evaluation are meticulously 
justified, aligning with the potential "Green 
effect" and the practical impact students can 
make. The 50% weightage to PHO acknowledges 
its significance in curbing controllable food 
wastage. EMD's 30% weightage reflects its 
substantial impact on carbon footprints, 
considering limited heavy appliances in hostels. 
The 20% weightage for Hostel Council Events 
stems from their direct influence on waste 
reduction during Zero Waste Days and the 
consequent fostering of enduring sustainable 
behaviors.

Annual Report: Sustainability Cell, IIT Bombay 2024-25 
Introduction 
The Sustainability Cell at IIT Bombay is a student-driven initiative committed to making the institute a 
model of sustainability. Our mission is to foster awareness, drive behavioral change, and implement 
impactful policy and technological innovations that align with global sustainability goals, such as the 
United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (UN SDGs) and the STARS certification framework. By 
collaborating with the administration, faculty, and students, we aim to integrate sustainability into 
every aspect of campus life. 
Structure 
The Cell is led by two Overall Coordinators, supported by three Managers and four Conveners per 
vertical, ensuring a robust and efficient organizational structure. The Cell operates through three 
specialized verticals: 
1.​ Projects and Policies: Focuses on research-driven initiatives, policy formulation, and 
implementation of impactful sustainability projects. This vertical works closely with GESH 
IITB (IIT Bombay Research Hub for Green Energy and Sustainability). 
2.​ Events and Operations: Organizes engaging events, competitions, workshops, and awareness 
campaigns while managing on-ground execution of projects. This vertical collaborates 
extensively with the Hostel Affairs Council to implement initiatives across hostels. 
3.​ Web and Design: Manages the Cell’s digital presence through creative designs, social media 
campaigns, and website updates to amplify outreach 
Initiatives in the Tenure 
 Strategic Framework Development 
1.​ STARS Certification Initiative (July 2024 - February 2025)   
The Cell conducted IIT Bombay’s first comprehensive assessment under the STARS 3.0 framework, 
analyzing over 500 parameters across academics, operations, and community engagement. 
Collaborating with GESH IITB and departments like PHO (Public Health Office) and EMD (Electrical 
Maintenance Division), we positioned IIT Bombay for a Silver rating based on preliminary analysis 
while identifying areas for improvement. 
 

 
2.​ Institute Sustainability Report (ISR)   
Published IIT Bombay’s first-ever ISR documenting contributions toward all 17 UN SDGs. The report 
highlighted key achievements in energy efficiency, waste management, water conservation, and 
biodiversity preservation while setting actionable goals for future projects. 
 
3.​ Sustainability Minor Proposal 
Drafted a proposal for a Minor in Sustainability and have received approval from relevant professors 
in the institute. The proposal highlighted the significance of introducing such a program for 
undergraduate students, outlined potential challenges, detailed the necessary administrative 
support, and presented a selection of potential courses for the minor. 
 

 
Green Cup 
The Green Cup is our flagship inter-hostel competition designed to promote sustainable practices 
across hostels. It incentivizes hostels to minimize their environmental footprint through categories 
like food waste reduction, electricity conservation, waste segregation, and participation in 
sustainability events. We received wide-spread support from the Deans and Professors for this 
initiative which includes: 
●​ Implementation: Monthly data was collected from sources like PHO for food waste metrics, 
EMD for electricity usage data, and hostel councils for participation in Zero Waste Days 
(ZWDs). Scores were calculated using transparent ranking systems for each category.  
●​ Activities: Hostels conducted Zero Waste Days to reduce mess waste generation and 
implemented innovative solutions like composting wet waste. Electricity consumption was 
normalized per capita or per room to ensure fairness in scoring.  
●​ Impact and Recognition: The competition fostered a sense of responsibility among hostel 
residents while creating measurable improvements in waste management and energy 
conservation practices. The Green Cup closing ceremony celebrated hostel achievements 
with awards based on cumulative scores. 
 
 

 
Technological Solutions 
4.​ Carbon Footprint Calculator   
Developed an intuitive carbon footprint calculator tailored for IITB students. Incorporating over 100 
parameters across Scope 1 (direct emissions), Scope 2 (indirect emissions from electricity), and Scope 
3 (indirect emissions from activities like commuting), this tool helps individuals understand their 
environmental impact. 
 
Carbon Footprint Calculator - IITB
 
5.​ Green Score Whitepaper  
Created a unique tool to calculate students’ "Green Scores" based on carbon emissions, water usage, 
and waste generation. A white paper detailing its methodology was submitted for faculty review. 
 

 
6.​ Green Fest Policy 
Created a policy proposal to push IITB’s fests (Mood Indigo, Techfest, E-Summit) to make a 
sustainable transformation. Identified key areas where environment-friendly practices can be 
adopted and provided best-practices from fests from across the world. Working towards 
implementing this in the next tenure. 
 
Campus Infrastructure Projects 
Drafted comprehensive policy proposals for on-ground improvements in campus sustainability. 
Several initiatives were seen to completion, some in pilot stages while others are at a proposal stage. 
7.​ AC Optimization in Lecture Hall Complex   
In collaboration with EMD, we piloted new door systems in LC001 to reduce cool air loss from air 
conditioning units. Initial estimates suggest up to a 15% reduction in energy consumption; actual 
impacts will be measured post-implementation. 
 

 
8.​ EV Buggy System Enhancement   
Strategically located EV buggy stops were established at high-traffic locations connecting academic 
areas with residential zones. Estimated improvements include up to a 30% increase in battery life; 
further data collection will validate these results. 
 
9.​ Liquid Waste Segregation Bins   
Piloted liquid waste segregation bins in Hostel 3 to improve biogas efficiency by reducing liquid 
content in food waste. Feedback from stakeholders informed design modifications for broader 
implementation. 
 
10.​Cycle Management System 
Proposed a centralized cycle ownership database using sticker identification to streamline transfers 
and promote reuse across campus. 
11.​Sustainable Construction Tenders Review 
Conducted a review of construction tenders for new hostels to identify sustainable practices that 
could be incorporated into future projects. 

 
Future Proposals - Sustainability Cell Implementation Proposals for GESH

common areas through collaboration with RUR Greenlife Pvt Ltd. 
13.​Energy Monitoring Systems   
Proposed implementing IoT-based smart meters in hostels to monitor wing-wise electricity usage in 
real time and identify inefficiencies. 
14.​On-Site Food Composters

such as irrigation or cleaning purposes. 
16.​Real-Time AQI Monitoring System   
Proposed installing AQI monitoring sensors at key locations on campus to track air quality levels 
year-round and promote better ventilation practices.

The Cell's website serves as a comprehensive platform for all its activities. It provides updates on

EDITORIAL NOTE

IIT BOMBAY AT A GLANCE
Indian Institute of Technology Bombay (IIT Bombay),
located in Powai, Mumbai, was established in 1958 and has
since grown into one of India’s top ranked engineering
institutions. Nestled over 550 acres amidst natural features
like lakes, wooded areas, and the adjacent Sanjay Gandhi
National Park, the campus environment reflects its
ecological ethos.
With 15 departments, numerous interdisciplinary programs,
and over 30 research centres, IIT Bombay is not only a hub
for cutting-edge academic research but also a model
institution in terms of integrating sustainability into its core
functions. The campus is home to more than 13,000 students
and is recognized globally for its emphasis on education,
research, and innovation.
From its early years, IIT Bombay has prioritized
sustainability as a central theme in campus planning and
institutional policy. It has implemented numerous eco-
friendly solutions such as:
Solar energy systems
Rainwater harvesting units
Energy-efficient building designs
Solid waste management and segregation programs
These initiatives help reduce the institute’s carbon footprint
while also serving as demonstrative models for other
academic and public institutions.
The institute’s approach to balancing technological
advancement with ecological responsibility reflects a
forward-thinking model of development that prioritizes both
innovation and the planet.

c. Student Led and Research Driven Sustainability
Some other prominent student-led bodies include:
Enactus -  Enactus IIT Bombay is a student-led social
entrepreneurship team that develops sustainable, market-ready
solutions to real-world challenges. Their key projects include
“SusPak”, which creates compostable packaging from
agricultural waste to replace single-use plastics; “Project
Ragini”, empowering rural women through ragi-based micro-
enterprises; and “Project Atithi”, promoting eco-tourism in
villages. By combining innovation with social impact, Enactus
IITB fosters scalable change through community-driven,
entrepreneurial action.
Zero Waste - Team Zero-Waste is a student-driven initiative
launched in 2024 under the Tata Centre for Technology and
Design, aiming to make the campus truly zero-waste-to-landfill.
The team works across three fronts - technology, policy, and
awareness, to design smart waste solutions, reduce single-use
materials, and promote sustainable behavior. From deploying
automated segregation systems to running plastic-free
campaigns and circular event plans, they blend innovation with
action. Notably, their intervention at the IITB Half Marathon
replaced 13,000 plastic bottles with clay kulhads, cutting
emissions and water waste while supporting local artisans.

These platforms cultivate a culture of hands-on learning and real-world problem-solving in
sustainability.
Team SHUNYA (Sustainable Housing for an Urbanizing Nation by
Young Aspirants) is IIT Bombay’s interdisciplinary student team
dedicated to designing affordable, net-zero, solar-powered
homes tailored for India’s urban population. Founded in 2012, the
team has earned global recognition through competitions like the
Solar Decathlon, recently winning 1st place in the 2024 U.S. Build
Challenge for their retrofit housing model “Project Samsara”.
Comprising students from across various departments and
guided by faculty, SHUNYA blends engineering, architecture, and
sustainability to promote green building and climate-resilient
housing.
Energy Club at IIT Bombay, is a student-driven interdisciplinary
body founded in 2011 to promote energy conservation and
sustainable development. Its mission is to transform the campus
into a model of “green energy efficiency” while driving innovation in
technology, policy, and awareness. The club conducts technical
workshops, policy discussions, and awareness campaigns, fostering
holistic engagement with energy transition challenges. Key
initiatives include “Renewathon”, a national-level challenge on real-
world energy problems; “Energize”, an energy-themed campus
scavenger hunt; and field visits to renewable energy sites. By
combining deep technical insight with community action, the club
builds a culture of environmental responsibility and forward-
thinking energy solutions.
Abhuday - Abhyuday is IIT Bombay’s student-run social
body, active from 2013-14, it focuses on civic
responsibility and youth-led impact. It organizes
awareness drives, cleanups, and its flagship Social Fest,
while also running national programs like the CR
Program and Social Fellowships. Fully student led, it
partners with NGOs and corporates to drive large-scale
change.

IIT Bombay has advanced multiple transformative initiatives that demonstrate
its leadership in environmental stewardship and innovation.The campus has
strengthened its green infrastructure through solar power plants, biogas
facilities processing residential food waste, rainwater harvesting and
greywater recycling systems, and GRIHA-rated energy-efficient buildings.
Sustainable mobility has been enhanced with electric shuttles, e-rickshaws,
cycling lanes, and expanded pedestrian pathways. Waste management has
become a benchmark practice, with campus-wide segregation, composting,
biogas generation, and recycling systems for plastics, e-waste, and biomedical
materials.
On the academic front, IIT Bombay is planning to introduce a Minor in
Sustainability, offering interdisciplinary courses that bridge policy, economics,
and technology to prepare students for the growing green economy. In
addition to this initiatives such as the Living Lab Fellowship, Greenovate
Challenge, and Green Cup 2.0 have fostered experiential learning and student-
led innovation in areas of energy, waste, water, and climate action.
Research excellence continues through the Green Energy and Sustainability
Hub (GESH), which drives innovation in renewable energy, sustainable
manufacturing, circular economy, and climate science. Faculty-led projects
have yielded pioneering solutions in water treatment, wetland-based sewage
systems, and carbon footprint monitoring, while industry collaborations and
global partnerships amplify impact and knowledge exchange.
Student-led bodies, Team Shunya, NSS, Abhyuday, Enactus, Zero Waste, and
the Energy Club, have expanded IIT Bombay’s sustainability footprint through
award-winning housing prototypes, large-scale plantation drives, zero-waste
initiatives, and nationwide outreach programs.
These efforts have translated into international recognition, with IIT Bombay
making a significant leap in the QS Sustainability Rankings, reinforcing its
position as a leading model of higher education sustainability in India. The
report reflects a campus-wide culture of innovation, responsibility, and
collaboration, positioning IIT Bombay at the forefront of advancing the UN
Sustainable Development Goals while shaping a resilient, net-zero future.

green economy is projected to reach $1 trillion by 2030, driven by
explosive growth in renewables, electric vehicles, green construction,

professionals with sustainability expertise are becoming increasingly
sought after.
Leading global university rankings now prioritize sustainability
education, directly impacting institutional reputation and student
career prospects. For IIT Bombay, a Sustainability Minor addresses real
job market trends while enhancing our institutional standing.

Event: 
Liquid Waste Segregation Bins
Body: 
Sustainability Cell
IIT Bombay’s liquid segregation bins initiative by the Sustainability Cell, mandates campus
wide at-source segregation of waste into dry (recyclables) and wet (liquid/biodegradable)
fractions in all hostels and residences. Specially designed bins capture liquid kitchen waste
separately from solid wet waste: kitchen staff first pour liquids such as leftover curries or
gravies into the designated liquid compartment, while food scraps go into the solid section.
Liquids are routed directly to appropriate composting or biogas plants, reducing excess
moisture in the solid waste stream. This segregation dramatically improves the efficiency of
composting and biogas production by ensuring optimal moisture content, enhancing gas
yields, and reducing operational issues like leachate. Pilots in Hostel 3 showed measurable
improvement in biogas plant performance and reduced contamination in recyclables. Ongoing
feedback from students and staff led to ergonomic design tweaks and simplified usage,
paving the way for smooth campus-wide adoption and setting a benchmark for institutional
waste management.
Event: 
Green Plumbing Project
Body: 
Team Shunya
This project incorporated water-saving fixtures, greywater recycling (collecting and treating
wastewater from sources like showers, sinks, and washing machines to reuse it for non-
potable purposes like toilet flushing and irrigation), rainwater harvesting, and solar-powered
hot water into sustainable housing prototypes. The initiative achieved up to 82% water savings
compared to conventional houses. By promoting circular economy practices and utilizing eco-
friendly materials, the project showcased scalable and affordable plumbing solutions, inspiring
sustainable urban housing approaches and raising awareness about water efficiency and
environmental responsibility in India.
Moreover, tap faucet aerators were proposed to be installed in hostel washrooms. Faucet
aerators work by mixing air into the water stream as it flows from the faucet, creating a
softer, more even flow while reducing water usage and splashing. This can be a promising
solution to reduce water consumption in hostels, without compromising hygine quality.

The policy shall be named as IIT Bombay Sustainability Policy. The policy shall
apply to the institution and all its Students, Post-docs, Staff, and Faculty. The
sustainability policy is aligned with the sustainable development goals (SDGs)
suggested by United Nations. There are seventeen verticals in SDGs which
consider technical, environmental and social aspects. By implementation of
this policy, IIT Bombay is firmly determined to achieve sustainability in all
domains of life at campus. Further, it will contribute definitely to spread of
sustainability principles out of the campus.  
Preamble
IIT Bombay is among the leading technical Institutes in India.
The institute is a residential campus with a current population
of almost 25000 (including floating mass). The campus is a
habitat of several native and seasonal biological species. The
campus is spread over a large biodiverse area. Due to increase
in demand for hostels, housing and academic facilities,
construction activities are also going on. In order to ensure
sustainable development, the current policy document is
prepared.

The purpose of the sustainable policy is to adopt green practices which
promote environment friendly ambience in the campus and develop sense of
responsibility among the residents towards environmental conservation. In
summary, the policy is committed to have high environment & social impact
and excellent Governance in the Institute. In addition, IIT Bombay considers
‘safety at working place’ as an important aspect to achieve sustainability.
Various sections/ departments across the Institute participate actively in the
above domains. To accomplish the purpose, following activities have to be
performed under different domains.

Among higher Education technical Institutions, IIT Bombay is among the few
Institutes which started study and research on environment in late seventies
and established a separate academic entity in the form of Centre for
Environmental Science & Engineering. Since then, the Centre is offering
M.Tech. and Ph.D. programmes. The ‘Centre’ has been upgraded as
‘Department’ almost five years ago since the inception of dual degree
programmes (B.Tech.-M. Tech. & MSc-PhD). In addition, other academic units
(such as Department of Energy Science and Engineering, Centre for Climate
Studies, Centre for Technology Alternatives for Rural Areas (CTARA),
Department of Humanities & Social Sciences and Centre for Policy Studies)
also offer several relevant courses related to environmental and social aspects
for the students admitted in different programmes.

Environment Studies which is mandated by Supreme Court of India, which
also covers sustainability concepts. The mandatory course is jointly offered by
Environmental Science & Engineering Department and Department of
Humanities & Social Sciences at IIT Bombay. Further, the online course content
will be made available for all campus residents.

(b) Water conservation and wastewater recycling
For water conservation and wastewater recycling, following measures will be
taken: 
1. Rainwater harvesting programmes will be executed. Some of the examples
may include construction of check dams (at feasible locations) at storm water
drains, groundwater recharging etc. 
2. Grey water from residential buildings will be treated and recycled as much
as possible. 
3. The stored rainwater and treated grey water will be used for various
purposes including horticulture activities, vehicle washing and toilet flushing,
depending upon the quality.

4. Provisions will be made to reduce water consumption. Some examples may

rooms, regular monitoring of water consumption etc. 
5. The supply pipes will be maintained and repaired to eliminate loss of water
due to leakages.
6. Water audits will be performed once in a year to identify the limitations and

(c) Solid waste treatment and recycling
Municipal solid waste collection and disposal will be performed in scientific
manner. 
1.  At source, waste segregation into dry and wet waste fractions is mandatory. 
2. All the wet biodegradable waste produced from houses and hostels is
processed using appropriate biological processes. 
3. The hostels’ messes have to reduce food wastage by taking suitable
measures.   
4. The not-readily biodegradable woody material may be processed to form
pellets within the campus if it is found feasible. 
5. The recyclable dry household waste will be recycled either in-house or
through an authorised recycler. 
6. Sanitary waste will be collected separately and disposed off through an
authorized biomedical waste collection agency. 
7. Single use plastics is banned in the campus. The buyers are encouraged to
carry recyclable bags. 
8. Waste plastic will be collected and recycled in proper manner.  
9. Biomedical waste from hospitals is being handled and stored as per
Biomedical Waste Management Rules (2016) before disposal through an
official agency. 
10. E-waste and discarded scrap material (such as old equipment, and their
parts, unusable furniture etc.) is being recycled and disposed by auctioning
through approved vendors only.   
11.  The excavated soil from various construction sites will either be reused at
the same site or will be utilised for horticulture activities in different grounds. 
12. Waste audits will be performed on regular basis to understand the
performance of existing practices and taking appropriate measures for further
improvement.
